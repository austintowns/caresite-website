# Review Round 2 -- Rural Single-Facility Administrator

**Persona:** Karen Hofstetter, Administrator of a 45-bed SNF in small-town Iowa. Solo handles marketing, compliance, and admissions. 6 Google reviews total (3 are old 1-stars). No prior reputation management tool experience. Found the site by Googling "how to get more nursing home reviews." Skeptical about spending $149/month on anything.

**Round 1 flags:** Too much jargon, price anxiety at $149/mo, setup overwhelm, trust issues with a startup.

---

## 1. What Improved

**SMS is now in the Basic plan.** This was my number one issue last round. The hero talks about SMS outreach, and the Basic plan at $149 now includes "SMS + email review campaigns." That removes the bait-and-switch problem entirely. I no longer feel tricked by the time I reach pricing.

**EHR language is broader.** The TrustBar now says "Works with PointClickCare, MatrixCare & all EHRs" right at the top of the page. The FAQ answer for "Does Caresite work with my EHR?" explicitly names PointClickCare, MatrixCare, Netsmart, and AHT, and says any EHR that can export a CSV will work. That is a real improvement. I use MatrixCare and I no longer feel like this product was not built for me.

**Several jargon terms got cleaned up.** The Features section now says "Review Volume Analytics" instead of "Review Velocity Analytics." The Automated SMS Outreach feature card explains the routing concept in plain English -- "Happy responders are guided to Google to leave a review. Unhappy responders go to a private form" -- without leading with the term "sentiment gating." That is exactly what I asked for. I can read that sentence and immediately understand the value.

**A phone number exists.** The TrustBar includes (435) 210-6111 and it is repeated in the footer. That was a hard requirement for me. I am not filling out a form and waiting for a stranger. I want to call.

**The contact form asks more useful questions.** It now includes your role (with Administrator as an option), number of facilities, and phone number. That tells me the conversation will be tailored to my situation instead of a generic sales deck.

## 2. Still Missing

**No small-facility testimonial or case study.** This was my number two request. Every testimonial is still from large or multi-facility operations: 120-bed Ohio, multi-facility group Texas, 90-bed Florida. The case study is a 120-bed facility in the Pacific Northwest. A 45-bed rural administrator still sees zero social proof from anyone who looks like her. The section header says "Trusted by facilities like yours" -- but none of these are like mine. That header actually makes the gap more noticeable.

**No company information.** I still do not know who runs Caresite. There is no About section, no team page, no founding story, no employee count, no customer count, no physical address beyond a phone number. The footer still only says "Caresite.io. All rights reserved." For a startup asking me to hand over my families' phone numbers, this is still a dealbreaker. The phone number helps, but I need to know there is a real company behind this.

**The "No BAA required" language is still unexplained.** The FAQ says "Because no PHI is handled, no Business Associate Agreement is required," but it does not address the obvious follow-up: I am uploading a list with family names and phone numbers. Whether or not that is technically PHI, my compliance officer is going to ask questions. A sentence like "We will sign a BAA if your compliance team requests one" or "Here is our data processing agreement" would go much further than "no BAA required," which still reads as "we do not want to sign one."

**The How It Works section still says "client-side parser."** Step 1 still reads: "Our client-side parser strips all PHI." I do not know what "client-side" means. I asked in Round 1 for this to say something like "your data is cleaned on your own computer before anything is sent to us." That specific fix was not made. The FAQ does not define it either.

## 3. New Issues

**The comparison table still assumes I am shopping between tools.** I said in Round 1 that I scrolled past this because I do not know who Podium, BirdEye, or Reputation.com are. The table is still there, still comparing features against those three competitors. For someone who is not yet sold on the category, this section adds length without adding value. It reinforces that this product is built for a buyer who already has a shortlist -- not someone who just Googled "how to get more nursing home reviews."

**The case study has no facility name or attribution.** "A 120-bed skilled nursing facility in the Pacific Northwest" is anonymous. If I am a buyer evaluating social proof, anonymous case studies carry very little weight. This is the same issue I raised about the testimonials -- first name plus last initial, no facility name, no verifiable information. The case study section is new and well-designed, but it reads as hypothetical rather than real.

**"Sentiment gating" still appears by name in the pricing and comparison table.** The Features section fixed this by leading with plain English, which I appreciate. But the pricing section still lists "Sentiment-gated review flow" (Basic) and "Sentiment gating with private feedback" (Standard) as line items. The comparison table lists "Sentiment gating" as a row label. A first-time reader who has not yet scrolled to the Features section or FAQ will see this term cold and not know what it means.

**TCPA is still unexpanded.** The TrustBar says "TCPA compliant" and the How It Works step 2 says "TCPA opt-out." Neither expands the acronym. I still do not know what TCPA stands for without looking it up. The Round 1 feedback specifically called this out.

## 4. Top 3 Remaining Changes

### 1. Add a testimonial or case study from a small, single-location facility (under 60 beds)

This is the single highest-impact change for someone like me. I need to see proof that this product works at my scale, in my kind of market, with my kind of budget. Right now every proof point on the page is from facilities 2-3 times my size or multi-facility operators. Even a short quote -- "We're a 40-bed facility in rural Missouri and we went from 3 reviews to 20 in our first 90 days" -- would do more to convince me than the entire comparison table. If that testimonial does not exist yet, that is a sign the product needs to earn it before marketing to this segment.

### 2. Add a brief "Who We Are" section or About blurb with real company details

This does not need to be a full About page. Even three sentences in the footer or above the contact form would help: who founded Caresite and why, how many facilities are currently using it, and where the company is based. The phone number was a good addition, but I still have no idea if this is a solo developer in a garage or a funded company with a support team. For a product that touches my facility's public reputation and my families' personal information, I need to know who I am trusting.

### 3. Replace remaining jargon: "sentiment gating" in pricing/comparison, "client-side parser" in How It Works, and expand TCPA on first use

Three specific text fixes: (a) In pricing, change "Sentiment-gated review flow" to something like "Routes happy families to Google, unhappy families to private feedback." (b) In How It Works step 1, change "Our client-side parser strips all PHI" to "Your data is cleaned on your own computer before anything is sent to us." (c) In the TrustBar, change "TCPA compliant" to "TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act) compliant" -- or better, just say "Text message compliance built in." These are small copy changes that remove the last friction points for a non-technical reader.

---

**Bottom line:** The pricing fix and EHR broadening were the two most important changes from Round 1, and both were done well. The page no longer feels like a bait-and-switch, and I no longer feel excluded for not using PointClickCare. What remains is a credibility gap: no proof this works for small facilities, no company identity behind the product, and a few stray jargon terms that remind me this page was written for a more sophisticated buyer. Fix those three things and I would fill out the form -- or more likely, call the number.
